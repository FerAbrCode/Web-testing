This page explains how to think about breaking news in prediction markets (like Polymarket) and how to turn a headline into a clear, testable market question.

Implications for Polymarket (general)

Breaking news typically moves markets because it changes the probability of an outcome, not because it is “good” or “bad.” The strongest price moves usually happen when news is both credible (verifiable) and surprising (meaningfully different from what traders expected).

Example (Jan 13, 2026): Ukraine’s parliament dismissed Vasyl Malyuk from the post of Head of the Security Service of Ukraine (SBU). A story like this can shift expectations about near-term announcements, institutional continuity, and what is likely to be confirmed (and when) by official channels.

A good reasoning process:

  • Start with the resolution rule: what exactly must happen for the market to resolve “Yes”?
  • Identify the causal chain: which facts in the story actually make “Yes” more likely (and which are just commentary)?
  • Separate signal from noise: prefer official documents and timestamped announcements.
  • Track base rates and alternatives: ask “what usually happens in similar situations?” and “what else could still happen?”
  • Re-check incentives and timing: upcoming events can add volatility even if the long-run probability is unchanged.

Source for the example item: Wikipedia current events (Jan 13, 2026).
